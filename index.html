Special Relativity Gravity & Time Can Time Run Backwards?

1. A 30-Second Overview

Albert Einstein's Theory of Relativity comes in two parts:

  • Special Relativity (1905) – shows that space and time are woven together into spacetime, and that moving clocks tick slower.
  • General Relativity (1915) – adds gravity, predicting that clocks in stronger gravity also run slower.

Combined, these ideas let different observers disagree about which events happen first. In extreme cases, the order can flip—making it look as if time ran backward. Below we break this down with numbers and cartoons you can play with.

3. General Relativity: Gravity Slows Time Too

Near a massive object of mass M at radius r, the (weak-field) slowing is Δt_far / Δt_near ≈ 1 + GM/(rc²). Use the slider to raise or lower an "elevator" around Earth.

4. How Can Time Appear to Run Backwards?

Relativity never makes your own clock tick backward, but it does let the sequence of two distant events swap for observers moving fast enough. Below is Einstein's train-and-lightning thought experiment.

Ground observer: bolts hit simultaneously.

Because simultaneity is relative, the passenger concludes the front bolt struck first. For them, the rear event lies in their "future," so the ground observer's ordering looks flipped—as if time reversed at the back of the train. This reversal is geometric, not magical: no observer ever violates cause-and-effect along their own timeline.

So, can we travel into our own past?

General Relativity admits exotic solutions (rotating black holes, wormholes) that contain closed time-like curves—paths that loop to the past. Whether nature allows them is unknown; most physicists suspect quantum effects shut the door. For now, "backwards time" remains a fascinating possibility, not an engineering blueprint.
